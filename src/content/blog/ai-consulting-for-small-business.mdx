# AI Consulting for Small Business: What It Actually Costs and What You Get

Most small business owners approach AI consulting the same way they approach buying software: they want a price, a timeline, and a promise.

The problem is AI consulting isn't software. It's process engineering. And the consultants who tell you exactly what they'll build before they've mapped your actual processes are telling you what you want to hear.

Here's what's actually happening in the market, what the pricing ranges mean, and how to run a 10-minute conversation that separates the real ones from the hype.

---

## What the Price Ranges Actually Cover

The range is wide because the scope is wide. Here's what you're actually buying at each tier.

**$997-$2,000/month (ongoing automation support)**

This is the retainer model. You get a dedicated consultant who maps your current processes, builds automations on tools like Make, Zapier, or n8n, and maintains them as your workflows evolve. Monthly calls, async support, and system updates included.

Who this is for: businesses spending 10-20 hours/week on repeatable tasks — customer follow-up, lead routing, report generation, scheduling. The math: if a $25/hour employee is doing 15 hours of automatable work per week, that's $1,500/month in labor on tasks that automation can handle for less.

**$5,000-$25,000 (project-based implementation)**

A defined project with a defined output. The consultant audits your current state, maps the target state, builds the automation stack, and hands it off with documentation. You own what's built.

Who this is for: businesses with a specific bottleneck — "we lose 40% of leads because follow-up takes 3 days" — where the outcome is clear enough to scope. Expect 4-8 weeks of actual work, not calendar time.

**$25,000-$150,000 (custom AI system implementation)**

Enterprise-adjacent scope. Custom AI models, complex integrations across your full tech stack, or AI-powered products rather than process automation. Most small businesses aren't here yet.

Who this is for: businesses with $2M+ revenue where a 10% efficiency gain on core operations is worth $200K. The math has to work at this scale.

## The Month 3 Problem

Here's what the pricing pages don't tell you.

Most AI automation projects fail between months 3 and 6, not in month 1.

Month 1 goes well. The automation runs. The consultant is excited. You're excited. The system handles the happy path — the normal lead, the typical order, the routine request.

Month 3 is when edge cases accumulate. The automation that works 90% of the time starts breaking on the 10% — the lead who fills out the form wrong, the order with a custom note, the customer who replies to the automated email with an actual question. Someone on your team starts manually handling exceptions. The exceptions become the workflow. The automation becomes irrelevant.

Good AI consultants build for edge cases before month 3, not after. They ask, during scope: "What are the weirdest things that happen in this process? What's the exception that your best employee handles differently than anyone else?"

Bad consultants build for the happy path and charge you to fix the edge cases separately.

Before hiring anyone, ask them: "Walk me through a project that had problems at month 3. What happened and what did you do?" Every experienced AI consultant has a month-3 story. The ones who don't haven't shipped enough to know what breaks.

## What Good Process Mapping Looks Like

AI consulting is 60% process mapping, 40% technology. Most consultants sell it as the inverse.

Before any automation gets built, a good consultant runs a process audit. This isn't a questionnaire. It's a working session where you walk through an actual example — a specific lead, a specific order, a specific support ticket — from start to finish.

In that walkthrough, they're looking for:

**Decision points.** Where does a human have to decide something, and on what information? Automating judgment is different from automating sequence.

**Exception handling.** Where does the process break or require special treatment? These are where automation dies if not built for.

**Data quality gaps.** What information would make the process run better if you had it, and why don't you have it? Often the automation opportunity isn't the process itself — it's capturing the data the process needs.

**Handoff latency.** Where does work sit waiting for someone to pick it up? Handoff latency is usually the highest-ROI automation target because it's pure wait time with no value creation.

A consultant who skips the process audit and goes straight to tools is building for what they know, not for what you need.

## The 10-Minute Interview

Before you hire anyone for AI consulting, run this conversation:

1. **"Tell me about an automation you've built. Not a category — a specific system."**
   Good answer: walks you through exact tools, integration points, what broke during setup, and what the actual time savings were. Bad answer: describes a category ("we've done CRM automation") without a specific example.

2. **"What AI project have you seen fail and why?"**
   Good answer: specific failure, specific cause, specific lesson. Bad answer: "we haven't had failures" or a vague reference to "client fit."

3. **"What's the first thing you'd want to understand about my business before recommending anything?"**
   Good answer: asks about process costs, current bottlenecks, team capacity to maintain new systems. Bad answer: asks about tech stack, budget, or timeline.

4. **"What won't AI solve for me?"**
   Good answer: clear, confident answer about where automation doesn't work — sales judgment, relationship-based decisions, anything requiring genuine creativity. Bad answer: can't name the limits.

A consultant who passes all four in 10 minutes is worth talking to about scope. One who fails two of four is going to cost you more than their fee in rework.

## When Not to Hire an AI Consultant

If your business is under $500K revenue and the primary constraint is customers, not operations — don't start with automation.

Automation makes efficient processes faster. It doesn't fix a sales problem, a product problem, or a market fit problem. Spending $997/month on process automation when your pipeline is empty is optimizing the wrong thing.

The right time to hire an AI consultant is when you can say specifically: "This process costs us X hours per week at Y cost, and if it ran faster/without errors, we'd capture Z in recoverable value."

If you can fill in those numbers, the math is worth running. If you can't, get the numbers first.

## FAQ

**How much does AI consulting cost for a small business?**
Ranges from $997/month for ongoing automation support to $10,000-$25,000 for project-based implementation. Hourly rates run $150-$350. The right structure depends on whether you need ongoing systems work or a one-time build.

**What does an AI consultant do for a small business?**
Maps your current manual processes, identifies the highest-ROI automation opportunities, builds the systems (or manages vendors who do), and maintains them as your business evolves.

**Is AI consulting worth it for small businesses?**
If you're spending 20+ hours per week on repeatable tasks — lead follow-up, data entry, scheduling, reporting — the math usually works at $997-$2,000/month.

**How do you know if an AI consultant is any good?**
Ask them about a specific automation they've built (not a category) and about a project that had problems. Experienced consultants have both stories. Hype merchants don't.
