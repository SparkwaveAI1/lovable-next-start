# How to Choose an AI Automation Partner (Without Getting Burned)

The AI automation space has a problem: everyone claims to do everything.

Every vendor has "cutting-edge AI." Every agency promises "10x efficiency." Every platform says it's "built for small businesses."

Meanwhile, you're a business owner trying to figure out who can actually help — and who's going to charge you $15,000 for a chatbot that doesn't work.

This guide is for the skeptic. The person who has been burned before, or who has watched others get burned, and wants a practical framework for evaluating AI automation partners.

---

## The Landscape: What You're Choosing Between

There are broadly four types of AI automation providers:

### 1. DIY Platforms (Zapier, Make, n8n)
**What they are:** Tools that let you build automations yourself using visual interfaces.
**Best for:** Teams with technical people who have time to build and maintain workflows.
**Watch out for:** They're tools, not solutions. You still need someone to design, build, test, and maintain everything. "Easy to use" doesn't mean "easy to get right."

### 2. Large Consulting Firms (Accenture, Deloitte, McKinsey)
**What they are:** Enterprise-grade strategy + implementation.
**Best for:** Companies with $500K+ automation budgets and complex multi-department needs.
**Watch out for:** They're expensive. Engagements start at six figures. They'll spend months on strategy before building anything. If you're a 20-person company, you're not their target customer.

### 3. AI Agencies (Freelancers, Boutique Shops)
**What they are:** Small teams that build custom AI solutions.
**Best for:** Specific, well-defined projects with clear scope.
**Watch out for:** Quality varies wildly. Some are excellent engineers. Some learned "AI" last month. Due diligence matters more here than anywhere else.

### 4. AI Automation Companies (Purpose-Built)
**What they are:** Companies that specialize in deploying AI automation for specific business outcomes.
**Best for:** Businesses that want outcomes, not projects. They want "faster lead response" not "build me a webhook."
**Watch out for:** Make sure they specialize in your kind of business. An automation company built for e-commerce may not understand service businesses.

## What to Look For

### 1. Outcome Focus vs. Feature Focus

**Red flag:** "Our platform has 200+ integrations and supports GPT-4, Claude, and 15 other models."
**Green flag:** "We'll cut your lead response time from 4 hours to 2 minutes."

Features are means to an end. The question isn't what technology they use. It's what business result they deliver.

Ask: *"What specific business outcome will this automation produce, and how will we measure it?"*

If they can't answer in plain business terms — revenue, time saved, cost reduced, customers retained — they're selling technology, not solutions.

### 2. Relevant Experience

**Red flag:** "We've built AI solutions across healthcare, finance, retail, manufacturing, education, and government."
**Green flag:** "We specialize in AI automation for service businesses doing $500K-5M. Here are three clients in your space."

Breadth is a warning sign. A company that serves every industry deeply serves none. Look for demonstrated experience with businesses similar to yours — similar size, similar model, similar challenges.

Ask: *"Can you show me a case study from a business like mine? What was the result after 90 days?"*

### 3. Speed to Value

**Red flag:** "Implementation takes 3-6 months. We start with a discovery phase..."
**Green flag:** "Your first automation will be live within two weeks. You'll see results in the first month."

For small businesses, a 6-month implementation is a death sentence. By month 4, the champion who approved the project has moved on, the business has changed, and the automation solves yesterday's problem.

Ask: *"How quickly will I see the first result? Not the first demo — the first actual business impact?"*

### 4. Transparent Pricing

**Red flag:** "Let's schedule a call to discuss your needs and build a custom proposal."
**Green flag:** "$997/month for the Growth plan. Here's exactly what's included."

Custom pricing isn't inherently bad, but opacity is. If a company won't tell you roughly what things cost until you're on a sales call, their pricing model depends on how much they think you'll pay, not how much the service is worth.

Ask: *"What does a typical engagement cost for a business my size? What's included, and what costs extra?"*

### 5. Ownership and Portability

**Red flag:** "Your automations live on our proprietary platform. If you leave, you start over."
**Green flag:** "We build on open standards. You own everything. If you want to leave, here's your data and your workflows."

Lock-in is the oldest trick in technology sales. If your automation only works inside one vendor's system, you're not buying a solution — you're renting one at their terms.

Ask: *"If I cancel tomorrow, what happens to my automations and data? Can I take them with me?"*

### 6. Support Model

**Red flag:** "Support is available via our ticketing system. Response time is 24-48 hours."
**Green flag:** "You have a dedicated point of contact. Response time is under 4 hours for business-critical issues."

When an automation breaks — and at some point, something will break — how fast can you get help? For automations tied to lead response, customer communication, or operations, a 48-hour ticket queue is unacceptable.

Ask: *"When something breaks on a Friday afternoon, what happens? Who do I call, and how fast will it be fixed?"*

## Red Flags That Should End the Conversation

1. **They can't explain what they do in plain language.** If every answer involves jargon, buzzwords, or technical specifications, they're either confused or hiding something.

2. **They promise results without understanding your business.** Anyone who guarantees outcomes before learning how your business actually works is selling snake oil.

3. **They push a long contract before proving value.** A 12-month commitment with a 60-day implementation timeline means you're committed for 10 months to something you haven't validated.

4. **They don't have references.** Every credible company has customers willing to talk about their experience. No references = no track record.

5. **They dismiss your concerns.** If you raise a worry and the response is "trust us" instead of addressing it directly — don't trust them.

---

## The Right Questions to Ask

Before signing anything, get clear answers to these:

1. What specific business outcome are we targeting?
2. How will we measure success?
3. What does the first 30 days look like?
4. What do you need from us to succeed?
5. What happens if it doesn't work?
6. Can I talk to a current customer?
7. What do I own if I cancel?
8. What's the total cost over 12 months — including hidden fees?

Any credible partner will answer all of these directly. Evasion on any question is a signal.

## How to Run a Smart Evaluation

### Phase 1: Define What You Need (1 week)
Before talking to anyone, write down:
- What business process is broken or too slow?
- What would "fixed" look like in measurable terms?
- What's your budget (monthly, not just setup)?
- What's your timeline for seeing results?

### Phase 2: Shortlist (1 week)
Research 3-5 companies that serve businesses like yours. Look for:
- Case studies in your industry or business model
- Clear pricing
- Recent customer reviews
- Content that demonstrates expertise (blog, guides, thought leadership)

### Phase 3: Evaluate (2 weeks)
Talk to each shortlisted company. Use the questions above. Ask for references. Compare:
- Speed to value
- Pricing transparency
- Cultural fit (do they understand your business?)
- Technical approach (does it make sense, even if you're not technical?)

### Phase 4: Pilot (4-8 weeks)
Start with one automation. One process. One measurable outcome. Don't commit to a platform-wide rollout until you've proven the value on a single use case.
