# How to Hire an AI Automation Company (Without Getting Burned)

The AI automation market has a problem.

Demand is growing fast. Supply is growing faster. And the gap between what companies promise and what they can actually deliver is wide enough to lose real money in.

Every web agency, consulting firm, and freelance developer has added "AI automation" to their services page. The result: business owners who know they need automation but can't tell the difference between a company that will transform their operations and one that will burn through their budget building something that doesn't work.

This guide is the filter. Here's how to evaluate an AI automation company before you write the check.

---

## The Three Types of AI Automation Companies

Before you start evaluating, understand what you're choosing between.

### Type 1: Tool configurators

These companies set up existing tools for you. They configure Zapier, Make, or similar platforms to connect your systems and create automated workflows.

**Best for:** Simple, well-defined automations (lead notification, data sync, email triggers). Typically $2,000-$10,000 for setup.

**Limitation:** You're limited to what the tools can do. When you need something custom, they hit a wall.

### Type 2: Custom AI builders

These companies build automation systems tailored to your specific business processes. They write code, train models, and create solutions that don't exist off the shelf.

**Best for:** Complex workflows, custom integrations, AI-powered decision-making. Typically $5,000-$50,000+ depending on scope.

**Limitation:** More expensive upfront. Quality varies dramatically between firms.

### Type 3: Full-service AI partners

These companies handle strategy, implementation, and ongoing optimization. They start by understanding your business, then design and build the right automation, then improve it over time.

**Best for:** Businesses that want a partner, not a project. Typically $1,000-$5,000/month ongoing.

**Limitation:** Requires a longer commitment. The value compounds over time, so short engagements miss the point.

Know which type you need before you start talking to vendors. If you hire a Type 1 company for a Type 2 problem, you'll get a solution that doesn't fit. If you hire a Type 2 company for a Type 1 problem, you'll overpay.

## The 7 Questions That Reveal the Truth

These questions separate the companies worth hiring from the ones that are good at selling.

### 1. "Can you show me an automation you built for a business like mine?"

Not a demo. Not a theoretical example. A real implementation for a real business in a similar industry or with a similar workflow.

**What good looks like:** They pull up a case study with specific metrics — time saved, conversion improvement, error reduction. They can describe the business context, what they built, and what the results were.

**Red flag:** Vague references to "similar projects" without specifics. Heavy reliance on hypothetical examples. They can't name a client or show real results.

### 2. "What happens when the automation breaks?"

Because it will. APIs change. Data formats shift. Edge cases appear. Every automation eventually encounters something it wasn't built for.

**What good looks like:** They have a monitoring system. They describe their error handling approach. They have a defined SLA for fixes. They can tell you about a time something broke and how they resolved it.

**Red flag:** They don't bring up failure scenarios. They oversell reliability. "Our automations don't break" is either a lie or a sign they haven't built anything complex.

### 3. "Who owns the work product?"

This matters more than most businesses realize. If the automation company goes out of business or you want to switch providers, can you keep what they built?

**What good looks like:** Clear contract language. You own the code, configurations, and integrations. They can export your workflows in a standard format. No vendor lock-in.

**Red flag:** They host everything on their proprietary platform. You can't export. If you stop paying, you lose the automation.

### 4. "What do the first 30 days look like?"

This reveals their actual process, not their sales pitch.

**What good looks like:** A clear onboarding process. Discovery phase (understanding your workflows). A defined first deliverable. A timeline. A point of contact.

**Red flag:** They want to start building immediately without understanding your business. No discovery process. The timeline is either impossibly fast ("live in a week") or impossibly vague ("depends on scope").

### 5. "How do you measure success?"

If they can't define what success looks like before they start building, they can't deliver it.

**What good looks like:** They ask about your business goals first. They propose specific metrics — response time, hours saved, error rate, conversion improvement. They set a baseline and a target before work begins.

**Red flag:** Success is defined as "the automation is running." No business metrics. No baseline. No targets.

### 6. "What's your team's AI experience?"

The market is full of companies that pivoted to "AI" without building real capabilities.

**What good looks like:** Team members with demonstrable AI/ML experience. They can explain how the AI components work in plain English. They've worked with the specific models and tools they're proposing for your project.

**Red flag:** Their "AI" is really just Zapier with a ChatGPT plugin. They can't explain the technology without marketing jargon. Their team is all sales and project management with no technical depth.

### 7. "What should I NOT automate?"

This is the most revealing question you can ask. A company that understands automation will tell you where it doesn't make sense.

**What good looks like:** They identify processes that are too variable, too high-stakes, or too low-volume to justify automation. They recommend starting with specific workflows and expanding. They're honest about limitations.

**Red flag:** They say everything can and should be automated. They agree with whatever you suggest. They never push back or set boundaries.

## Red Flags That Should Kill the Deal

Beyond the evaluation questions, watch for these warning signs:

**No fixed-price option.** Good automation companies can estimate scope and price it. "We bill hourly and it depends" usually means the scope isn't understood and costs will balloon.

**They lead with technology, not problems.** If the first conversation is about AI models, frameworks, and technical architecture instead of your business challenges, they're building what they want, not what you need.

**Long timelines with no milestones.** "We'll deliver in 6 months" without defined checkpoints means you won't know it's failing until you've spent the full budget.

**No references.** Any reputable automation company has clients willing to speak about their experience. If they can't provide references, they either don't have happy clients or don't have clients at all.

**They promise the moon.** "AI can handle 100% of your customer service" or "You'll never need to hire again." If it sounds too good, it is.

## What to Expect in Pricing

Honest pricing ranges for AI automation services in 2026:

**Simple automations (connect and trigger):**
- $1,000-5,000 one-time setup
- $100-300/month for maintenance and monitoring
- Timeline: 1-2 weeks

**Custom workflow automation:**
- $5,000-25,000 for initial build
- $500-1,500/month for maintenance, monitoring, and optimization
- Timeline: 4-8 weeks

**Full-service AI automation partnership:**
- $1,000-5,000/month (strategy + build + support)
- Includes ongoing optimization and expansion
- Timeline: First automation live in 2-4 weeks, expanding monthly

**Enterprise / complex implementations:**
- $25,000-100,000+ for initial build
- $2,000-10,000/month for ongoing support
- Timeline: 2-6 months

If someone quotes dramatically outside these ranges, ask why. Below-market pricing usually means corners cut on quality. Above-market pricing might be justified for complex work, but should come with proportional capabilities.

## The Evaluation Process: A Practical Template

**Week 1: Shortlist**
- Identify 3-5 companies that match your type and budget
- Review their websites, case studies, and client testimonials
- Check their work on LinkedIn — do they have real people with real AI experience?

**Week 2: First conversations**
- 30-minute discovery call with each
- Ask the 7 questions above
- Evaluate how well they listen vs. how much they pitch

**Week 3: Proposals**
- Request a written proposal from your top 2-3
- Proposal should include: scope, timeline, pricing, success metrics, team members
- Compare specificity — vague proposals = vague execution

**Week 4: Decision**
- Check references (2 per finalist)
- Evaluate cultural fit — you'll be working with these people for months
- Choose based on capability, clarity, and track record — not just price

## What a Good Engagement Looks Like

**Month 1:** Discovery and first automation. The company learns your business, identifies the highest-impact starting point, and deploys your first automation. You see results within 30 days.

**Month 2-3:** Expand and optimize. Based on Month 1 data, they build the next automations. The first one gets refined based on real performance data.

**Month 4+:** Steady state. Automations are running, monitored, and continuously improved. Your team has adapted to the new workflow. You're seeing measurable ROI.

**Throughout:** Regular check-ins, transparent reporting, and clear communication about what's working and what isn't.

If an engagement doesn't follow this pattern — if you're three months in with nothing deployed — something went wrong at the hiring stage.
