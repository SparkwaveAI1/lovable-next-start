# The Lead Response Window: Why 5 Minutes Is Already Too Late

A new lead came in this morning.

They submitted a form, sent a text, or left a voicemail. They're thinking about their problem right now — in this moment, they're motivated, they have the issue top of mind, and they're willing to talk to whoever gets back to them first.

How long until you respond?

If the answer is "a few hours" or "whenever I see it," you're not in the running. The window isn't a few hours wide. It's a few minutes.

---

## The Research Is Clear

Harvard Business Review published data showing that companies that respond to leads within an hour are **7x more likely to qualify the lead** than those who respond even one hour later.

Separate research from Lead Response Management found that calling within the first 5 minutes makes you **100x more likely to reach the prospect** compared to calling 30 minutes later.

By the time most service business owners call back — 4, 6, 8 hours after the inquiry — the prospect has moved on. They've talked to someone else. They've made a decision. Or they've simply cooled off and are no longer in buying mode.

You didn't lose the job because your price was wrong. You lost it because someone else was faster.

---

## Why This Is Especially Hard for Service Businesses

A roofing contractor, HVAC tech, or landscaping company owner isn't sitting at a desk waiting for leads to come in. They're on a job. Under a roof. In someone's mechanical room. Driving between sites.

The lead comes in at 2pm. They see it at 5pm. They call at 5:30pm.

By then, the homeowner has already booked someone else — or at minimum had a full conversation with a competitor and is now comparing your cold call against a warm relationship they just built with someone who called them back in two minutes.

The problem isn't work ethic. It's systems.

---

## What a 2-Minute Response Changes

When a lead gets a response in under 2 minutes, several things happen:

**They haven't moved on yet.** They're still in research mode, still thinking about their problem. You're catching them at peak motivation.

**You're first.** Even if they called three companies, you're the first voice they hear. First impression advantage is real.

**The response feels personalized.** A fast, specific reply — one that references what they actually inquired about — signals attentiveness. It's a proxy for how well you'll handle their job.

**You set the frame.** First contact defines the relationship. Being responsive from minute one establishes an expectation of professionalism before you've ever met.
